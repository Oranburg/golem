AI, the Golem, and the Limits of a Ban

The same argument for a wider readership: the machines we cannot forbid and cannot put down, and the old law of who answers.

AI, the Golem, and the Limits of a Ban

In the old story, a rabbi shapes a man out of river clay and breathes a word into him, and the clay stands up and works. The creature in the legend has no soul and no speech. It does what it is told and a little more, and when it grows dangerous the rabbi rubs out a letter and it falls back into mud. The Jews of Prague and of Chełm told this story for centuries before anyone built a machine that could fly itself to a target, and they were already worried about the thing that matters now: who answers for what a made thing does.

An artificial system is a golem in that old and exact sense. It is a thing a person animates that acts in the world and cannot answer for what it does. So when Pope Leo XIV writes, in his encyclical Magnifica Humanitas, that it is not permissible to entrust lethal or otherwise irreversible decisions to artificial systems, he is reaching for something real. There is a good in a person being the one who decides to take a life, and an encyclical that reaches for it is not wrong to reach. The trouble comes one step later, when the sentence names that worry and then issues a prohibition without giving anyone the rule that would say which decisions fall on the forbidden side and which do not.

Set two machines beside each other. A CyberKnife fixes a beam of radiation on a tumor and burns it away, and what it burns does not grow back. A loitering munition circles a battlefield, picks out a target, and dives into it, and that cannot be undone either. Both make a decision that is lethal. Both make a decision that is irreversible. If “lethal or otherwise irreversible” were the principle, it would forbid the one we are glad to have in a hospital along with the one we are right to fear on a battlefield. The two words name a feature the machines share, which is the one thing they cannot do, tell the machines apart.

The hard cases are worse, and they are real, not hypothetical. Along the Korean Demilitarized Zone, South Korea has fielded a sentry gun called the SGR-A1. It watches the fence with cameras and heat sensors, calls out a challenge, and is built to open fire if the intruder gives no code. Whether it fires on its own or only when a soldier presses the button is disputed by the very people who built it: the company says a human authorizes every shot, and the lead engineer told a reporter that the robot has an automatic mode in which it can make the decision. The honest answer is that no one outside the program knows, and the units that are deployed are classified. There are a thousand machines like it in the middle of the spectrum, between the surgeon’s beam and the killer drone, and neither “lethal” nor “irreversible” sorts a single one of them.

A defender of the encyclical will say the real rule is narrower: do not hand the machine the judgment of which target to strike. That is a serious idea, and it sorts the first two cases cleanly. The surgeon decides and the CyberKnife carries it out; the drone decides for itself. But it breaks on the sentry gun. A human wrote the rule, that anyone who crosses this line under arms is a target, and the machine applied that rule to the person standing in front of it. Did the human make the decision, or did the machine? Every autonomous system has that shape: a human writes the category, the machine picks the instance, and the deciding is smeared across the gap between them. No one has said where along that gap the human stops being the one who decided, because the gap is in the thing, not in the words.

There is a further trouble, and it is not a lawyer’s quibble. A flat prohibition needs to name what it forbids, and “artificial intelligence” is not a thing you can name. A toaster is sold with AI. A car’s guidance computer decides. If artificial intelligence means a machine that makes an automated choice, then almost nothing on a modern assembly line is free of it, and if it means a large language model, then the targeting chip in the drone is not artificial intelligence at all. You cannot categorically forbid what you cannot define, and the common law never tried. Its oldest categories of harm are named for what a thing does, the ox that gores, the pit left open, the fire that spreads, not for what the thing is made of. The golem is that kind of category. It is defined by what it is, a thing that acts and cannot answer, and it does not care whether the clay is river mud or silicon.

So I went to the old sources looking for the missing rule, and I found something better than a rule. I found a model of how law behaves when the rule cannot be written. The Jewish law of capital judgment is built around what a court can actually know with the certainty that killing a man demands. For a court to put someone to death, the killing has to be by the killer’s own hand, with two witnesses, after a warning, and never on inference. Where the court cannot reach that bar it does not pretend. It says the man is exempt in the human court and answerable to Heaven, and it hands what it cannot judge to a forum without those limits. The same tradition calls a court that executes once in seventy years a hanging court. Notice what it is doing with the very thing the encyclical fears. Its own death sentence is the irreversible act it cares about most, and it meets that irreversibility not with a ban but with a higher standard of proof and a humble retreat. When the stakes cannot be undone and the judgment cannot be made with the needed care, the wise move is to raise the bar and be honest about what is left over, not to forbid the act and pretend the line is clear.

And we cannot simply put the weapons down. It would be good to beat these swords into plowshares, but artificial intelligence will not be disarmed, not with this technology and not now, and wishing the hard cases out of existence is not a policy. So the two easy answers are both closed. We cannot write the categorical rule, because we cannot state its principle or name its subject, and we cannot renounce the thing, because it will not be renounced.

What is left is the older instrument, and I think it is the honest one. The common law of foreseeable harm asks, case by case, whether a responsible person set the force in motion and whether the harm was the kind he was bound to expect, and it runs the answer back to him. The Talmud says the same when it treats a man’s fire as his own arrow: once you loose it you answer for where it lands, and the reason it is still your arrow is that it has left your hand. The keeper of an animal known to be dangerous answers in full, because he knew what it would do. The operator of a machine whose behavior is engineered and known in advance stands exactly there.

The strangest turn is that the corporation got here first. A corporation is a golem too, conjured by filing words with the state and dissolved by filing more, with no soul of its own, acting only through the people inside it. The law has always handled it by finding those people and running the responsibility back to them, and by lifting the corporate veil when the company was only a shell. But take the people out, run the firm on software, or build it as one of the new decentralized autonomous organizations that are nothing but code, and the corporation becomes a golem all the way down. There is no longer anyone inside to answer. That is the same gap the autonomous weapon opens, arriving in a business suit, and the courts are already feeling for the old rule: when a decentralized organization could not answer for itself, a federal regulator reached past it and held its members, the way the law has always reached the human behind the golem.

So the Pope and I can both be right, and two careful readers can land in different places without either being careless. We may simply not share our first principles. If you believe some lines must be drawn even before they can be administered, you will read the encyclical one way. If you believe a rule we cannot administer does more harm than the case left to judgment, you will read it another. The encyclical names a value that may well be sound. I am pointing out the absence of the rule that would make it workable and the absence of the exit that would make it moot, and neither of those cancels the value. I remain open to the principle. If anyone can state the rule that tells the CyberKnife from the killer drone in terms a court or a treaty could apply, I will follow it. Until it arrives, the doctrine that controls is older and simpler. No technology is strictly forbidden, but the one who sends it answers for what it foreseeably does.
